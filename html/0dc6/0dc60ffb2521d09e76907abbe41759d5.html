The Basic Problems of the Chinese People's Republic in the Transition Period and the Requirements of the Flrst Mw-Tear Plan In 1949 the Chinese people under the leadership of the Chinese C6mmuriiat Party and its great leader, comrade Mao Tse-tung, completed the first stage of the Chinese revolution, ousted the imperialist forces from the continent of China, overthrew the comprador bureaucratic bourgeoisie and its power, established a democratic people's dictatorship based on the alliance of workers and peasants under the leadership of the working class which com- bines the various democratic strata, and created the Chinese People's Republic. Thus the fundamental question of the revolution, the question of power, has been solved. Following the establishment of the Chinese People's Republic, the Communist Party of China and the central people's government immediately carried out a number of extremely difficult measures on the economic front. To begin with, the centralisation of financial and economic operations on a countrywide scale was implemented. The country's income and expenditures were balanced, inflation was halted, prices were stabilised, and a very energetic agrarian reform was put into effect. Much work was done toward the restoration of the national economy which had been subjected to severe devastation under the domination of the Kuomintang reactionaries; a radical change was achieved In the financial and economic improvement of the country. When the restoration of the national economy was completed, the Communist Party of China lost no time in providing a precise definition of the basic problems of the state in the transition period; it -%osed those problems before the people of the entire country. These problems are defined in the constitution as follows. "The period between the creation of the Chinese People's Republic and the building of a socialist society is a transition period. The basic problems of the state during the transition period are the gradual implementation of the country's industrialisation and the gradual completion of the socialist changes in agriculture, handicrafts industry, and capitalist industry and trade." With a view to dealing with the historical problems of.the country during the transition period, the central people's government, basing its decision on the state of the national economy prior to its final restoration, outlined the task of planned economic construction to begin in 1953 and officially published the First Five-'car Plan for the develop- ment of the national economy (1953-1957) in.1955. The economic basis of China's national economy is still a backward one. In 1952 the value of the overall output of the modern part of industry was only 26.7% of the total output of the industry and agri- culture; although China in 1952 attained or surpassed the previous highest production level in its history in regard to basic industrial products. China's heavy industry was still very weak; for example, the annum production of steel in 1952 was only 1.35 million t,-pig iron 1.9 mill'an t, electric power 7.26 billion kwh, and oil 436,000 t. we were unable to produce large machines and automobiles. Small, individually- owned farms predominated in agriculture. Socialist industrialisation will create a base for China's further development. Foreign Trade Problems Foreign trade is one of the integral, inalienable parts of China's national economy. According to the basic requirements of the First Five-Tear Plan of economic construction, the implementation of.foreign trade functions of exports and imports will guarantee the realization of the Five-Tear Plan. The most important problem of the First Five-Tear Plan is the establishment of a heavy industry base. It would be very difficult to establish such a base within a short period of time if we were to rely only on the resources and equipment now available in the crrvntry. To accelerate the construction tempo of Chinas industry, we must utilise every possibility including the possibility of purchasing abroad electrical equipment, machine tools, machires, and other goods necessary for the establishment of Chinas heavy industry base which we are not yet able to produ-v at all or cannot produce in sufficient quantities. In addition to heavy industry, we must also develop transportation, communications and light industry in a proportional degree in the First Five-Tear Plan. These branches of the economy are short of a number of types of production machinery; some of them can be produced in the country but in quantities far from sufficient to meet the current requirements, and others cannot yet be produced at all. Certain types of industrial raw materials, though already produced in the country, cannot yet fully meet the raw material requirements of the daily growing branches of industry. In view of ttis, the existing requirements of the mentioned types of equipment and industrial raw materials are met through imports. Under way now in China is a powerful movement for the cooperative type of agriculture; agricultural cooperatives are developing rapidly and the production enthusiasm of the pea::ants has risen to new heights. The changes taking place in production relations have produced the need for new agricultural equipment. Particularly great are the requirements for mineral fertilizer. And since domestic production is still" unable to supply this product in sufficient quantities, the existing requirements must be met through imports. All this shows that the main purpose of imports is, first of all, to facilitate the development of heavy industry as well as transportation, light industry, agriculture, and other important branches of China's national economy. Of great importance during the Five-Tear Plan also is the importation of certain types of essential consumer goods such as kerosene, medicines, sugar, cloth, and so on. In order to import, China must export to a corresponding degree. Although China's national economy is still' backward, the country turns out a substantial quantity and variety of agricultural, animal husbandry, and mining industry products as well as those of the handicrafts industry. Such Chinese products as tea, beans, tang oil, bristles, wolfram ore, natural silk, and silk products have long been famous on the foreign market. Following the liberation, the production of most of these goods very rapidly reached the highest prewar level, while the production of some of them exceeded that level. On the other hand, aa'a result of the rapid development of a number of light industry branches, the production of many types of industrial goods has been increasing gradually. However, in view of the present domestic market capacity, some of these goods are not required in the quantities in which the.* are produced, while some of the other goods, which are-In great demand, can be used more economically, thereby increasing the quantities for export purposes. All this constitutes a material base for the export of goods. That is why much initiative should be shown in the export of these goods. Since it is possible to pay for imports with the foreign currency realised from the sale of exports, these particular branches of production should be supported and developed. This shows that the developee': of exports is a very important. task and that the problems in this field are enormous. The importance of foreign trade is not limited to the fact that it greatly contributes to the solution of the domestic problems facing the country. Since foreign trade is an international exchange of goods, it also plays an important role in the solution of problems connected with foreign rplationt. The Chinese People's Republic has already established economic ties of an entirely new type with the Soviet Union and all people's demo- cracies. Acting on the principles of internationalism, the Soviet Union is offering the Chinese People's Republic disinterested, all-round, and long-term assistance. Thanks to this aid, the construction of China's socialist economy is being carried out at a rapid pace. The people's democracies are also giving China great economic assistance. The Chinese People's Republic, in turn, does everything possible to support the economic construction in the countries of the socialist camp with the shipments of its various products. This whole-hearted assistance and close cooperation in the field of economy are the basis of prosperity of the democratic world market, an important factor contributing to the general economic upswing of all the t.rother countries and to the strengthening of peace in the whole ww>rld. The first important international problem in China's foreign trade is the development and consolidation of the economic ties with the countries of the socialist camp. The Chinese People's Republic is successfully building a socialist system of economy. However, proceeding from the principle of the peaceful coexistence of 2 systems expounded by Lenin, the great teacher of the revolution, China has been and still is engaging in active trade with the capitalist countries since the establishment of it. new govern- ment. The capacity of the Chinese People's Republic market-is very great. We hope to buy the various goods we need on the capitalist markets. The Role of Loreisn Trade in t.> Development of the National =ooncwm Thanks to the correct leadership of the Communist Party of China and the central people's government of the Chines People's Republic, the country's foreigr, trade problems are being successfully solved. In the first 3 years following the liberation of China. its foreign trade played a big part in the restoration of the national economy. In the next 3 years it also played an active part in the implementation of the plans for the first 3 years of the First Live Tear Plan for national economic development. China's foreign trade turnover has been increasing from year to year. Taking the 1950 foreign trade turnover as 100%, the 1953 turnover jumped to 181%. In 1954 China's foreign trade turnover was more than double the 1950 volume; in the first 3 quarters of 1955 it was 15.1% larger than during the corresponding period of 1954.' According to the Five-Tear Plan, the 1957 foreign trade volume is to be 56.5% greater than in 1952. The steady expansion of China's foreign trade volume reflects the upswing of industrial and agricultural pro- duction in the country as well as the further strengthening of its economic ties with different countries of the world. It should be pointed out that China's exports and imports have been substantially balanced since 1950. This shows that the ties between China's national economy and that of other countries are q site normal and sound. The conditions characteristic of old China under which imports exceeded exports over long periods of time have been eliminated for all time. The part of foreign trade in the national economy is determined not so much by the quantitative vol,,- of imports and exports as by the type of the imported and exported goods. The bulk of China's imports over a number of years has consisted of means of production. Only 10% of it consists of consumer goods. In China's imports of producer goods the value of machine aggregates has been increasing from year to year. The importation of sets of equip- ment in 1951 was more than 33 times greater than in 1950. All this is as different as day from night when we compare the situation to the conditions prevalent in old China when the imports of large quantities of consumer goods and luxury items led to the stagnation and ruin of domestic industry enterprises. Agricultural products are the chief items of Chinese exports. The sale of exportable products by the peasants has greatly increased their purchasing power. This made it possible for them to acquire increasing quantities of the production facilities needed for the development of their economy as well as essential industrial goods which insured a higher living standard for the rural population and, to a considerable degree, consolidated the alliance of workers and peasants. It should be pointed out that the volume of China's exported industrial goods has been appreciably increased in the past 1-1/2 to 2 years. It is already possible to export large quantities of such industrial goods as cotton cloth, steel wire, nails, thermos bottles, and certain other items. In 1955 China began to export to many Southeast Asian countries a variety of industrial goods which had never been exported in the past: thin sheet metal, steel of various shapes, cast iron pipes, potassium chloride, white zinc, stained paper, and others. Changes in Foreign Trade Distribution by Countries The changes in foreign trade distribution by countries is of enormous importance to new China. When the Chinese People's Republic was formed, the single world market broke up and a new democratic world market emerged next to the capitalist one. The Chinese People's Republic became one of the major members of the democratic world market. Under the old Chinese regime, the volume of USSR trade with China was unusually small. In the 20 years between 1917 and 1936, the Soviet Union's trade with China reached its peak in 1928 when it amounted to only 5.41% of China's total foreign trade; in some years it was even below one percent. China's trade with Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Rumania, and the other countries of East Europe was quite insignificant prior to China's liberation. New China's trade with the Asian people's democracies has also been rapidly developing in recent years. China, the Korean Democratic People's Republic, and the Democratic Republic of Vietnam are brother countries which share their joys and troubles with each other and are tied together in friendship which was tested in hardship. The Korean and Vietnam peoples have already made an outstanding contribution to the cause of the struggle against imperialism and for national liberation and independence. Their struggle is of great importance to the Chinese people; that is why China's relations with the Korean Democratic People's Republic and the Democratic Republic of Vietnam have been growing closer from year to year. China's economic ties with the Mongolian People's Republic have also been growing stronger from year to year. The comparatively recently com- missioned China-Mongolia-USSR railroad has produced good opportunities for the further development of Chinese-Mongolian economic relations.. China's trade with a number of Southeast Asian countries - India, Ceylon, Burma, Indonesia, and Pakistan -- has also been steadily expanding in recent years. Chinas trade relations with these countries have been steadily expanding. since the Bandung Conference of 1955. The recently signed trade agreement between China and Egypt laid the foundation for the normal development of Chinese-Egyptian trade. China's trade relations with the countries of Western Europe have been gradually restored and the trade volume with these countries has been increasing from year to year since the 1952 Moscow Economic Con- ference. This shows that not only China is actively working for broader economic ties with the Western European countries, but also that the industrialists of those countries are greatly interested in trade with China. Incidentally, those who are trading with China have already been convinced of the advantages of this trade.
