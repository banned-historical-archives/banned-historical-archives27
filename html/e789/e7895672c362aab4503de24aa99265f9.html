F77 I INDIA: Ghandi's Position Stronger The Indian Government's recent actions against for- mer Prime Minister Gandhi appear likely to result, at least temporarily, in strengthening her position. I I Gandhi was arrested on Monday on charges that in- cluded taking a bribe for granting an oil drilling contract to a French firm. The government may have expected the initial hearing to be pro forma, but Gandhi refused bail and the mag- istrate released her unconditionally on the grounds that the government had presented no evidence to support its charges. Home Minister Charan Singh--who probably pushed hardest for action against Gandhi--later admitted to the, press that there was no proof against her in the bribery case:. Gandhi has accused the government of conducting a vendetta. All factions of the opposition Congress Party have announced their support for her, and the incident may well al- low her to dominate a special session of the party's ruling body to be held next week. Although some ruling party officials believe the government erred in bringing the charges, the government now seems to have little choice but to pursue the case and is ap- pealing her release to a higher court. The incident has clearly hurt the credibility of the government, but it still appears unlikely that its control of parliament will be affected. Charan Singh has probably been hurt most by the case , but he is far too strong to be removed from office easily.
