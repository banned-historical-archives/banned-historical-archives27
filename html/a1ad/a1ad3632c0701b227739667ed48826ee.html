For the past two weeks, the Soviets have been select- ing commentary from the world press and using their own edito- rials to portray neutron weapons as inhumane and as an impedi- ment to further progress on arms limitation. Pravda has cited the weapon as a case study of "secret" US policymaking, and Tass said yesterday that the issue would hamper the mutual and balanced force reduction talks. Other Soviet commentary has charged that deployment of neutron weapons would be contrary to decisions reached at the Helsinki conference. Enhanced-radiation weapons, moreover, would provide US forces in Western Europe with increased flexibility, partic- ularly in view of Moscow's general lack of passive defense against a neutron warhead for its tactical field forces. Thus far, the increased criticism of US weapons pro- grams a not altered an apparent Soviet decision to tone down personal criticism of President Carter. For the past several months, the Soviets have been painting a highly negative pic- ture of the President's intentions. More recently, however, the Soviet attacks have been against US policies rather than the US President, particularly on issues related to SALT and the Middle East. Apparently some Soviet leaders believe that the almost unrelieved negative assessment of the Carter administration's intentions was counterproductive, although there has apparently been no decision to change the substance of Soviet accusations on key policy-related issues. USSR: Fisheries Agreements with LDCs anxious to bypass constraints on its fish- The USSR , ing industry imposed by the 200-mile territorial zones declared by various countries, has continued to expand its fisheries assistance program in the Third World. New agreements in the past 18 months with seven developing countries--mostly to create joint fishing ventures--bring the total number of such Soviet fisheries accords to 38. These agreements give the USSR the most widespread and largest fisheries assistance program among the developing countries. They also provide Moscow with fishing concessions and logistic support for its vast fishing fleet at relatively small cost. Approved F Approved For I Initially, the USSR viewed fisheries assistance as a men.'Avis o satisfying the requirements of its own far-flung fleet. By providing these countries with extensive port and processing facilities, trawlers, fishing gear, and training, the Soviets have obtained reciprocal services and access to port facilities, prolonging the fleet's time at sea. In recent years, the USSR has increasingly preferred to engage ,in joint ownership arrangements in which 49 percent of the investment is Soviet. Because 60 percent of the Soviet catch comes from within 200 miles of the coasts of other coun- tries, the USSR has felt the need to guarantee its long-term access to developing countries' waters and to secure a voice in their national fisheries policies. Through the :joint ventures, the developing countries receive technical services, training, and research facilities with minimum outlays. They also obtain modern facilities through lease or purchase from the Soviets. I More recently, Moscow has negotiated commercial joint ventures designed to provide Soviet fishing vessels with ship- chandling services and to aid in processing, freezing, and marketing the Soviet catch. These ventures are located in Singa- pore, Spain, and the Philippines. Fransov, a joint Soviet-French company established in 1975, acts as an agent for the Soviet fleet in obtaining fishing rights in waters of countries where direct Soviet access has been politically impossible. Fransov also markets some of the Soviet fleet's catch from African waters, registers Soviet trawlers in developing countries, and may be managing construc- tion of Soviet shore facilities in several countries. I The use of joint commercial companies, such as Fransov, appeals Jto some governments because they can receive the benefits of an increased fish catch as well as profits from sales and yet not a the political cost of direct association with Moscow. Yugoslavia: The Soviet delegation to the Belgrade CSCE meeting continues to insist upon a firm closing date for the fall review conference, thereby reducing the likelihood of an early end to the current session. At an evening meeting on Wednesday, the Soviets sharply reiterated, their refusal to accept the Western and neutral position that the duration of the fall meeting be determined by consensus.
