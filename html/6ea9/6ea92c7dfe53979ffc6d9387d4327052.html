Int odu.ction On 6 January 1958, the Standing Committee of the National People's Congress (NPC) announced that the Fifth Session of the Congress would be held in Peking, beginning on 25 January. This announcement was somewhat unexpected since the Fourth Session had been held as recently as 26 June - 15 July 1957, and normally a period of a year had been allowed to elapse between such meetings. The agenda for the Congress as announced on 6 January included (a) the national economic plan for 1958, (b) the examination and approval of the 1958 budget, and (c) the approval of the alphabetization of the Han language. This agenda made it appear that important economic problems and decisions had required the convening of the Congress in advance of the norr.lal date in order to secure its formal approval. Recent events in Communist China, however, led some to won- der whether there was not at least one other question which, even though not mentioned in the announced agenda, might engage the attention of the Congress, During the preceding months, a number of provincial congresses had been taking action to dismiss from party and government posts in the provinces rightist elements which had been identified in the course of the "blooming and contending" campaign. It seemed plausible that one reason for the convening of the Fifth Session of the NPC might be to carry the process of punish- ment and ptvrging of rightists to thezational level. For nearly three weeks after the 6 January announcement no further public mention was made of the forthcoming Fifth Session. No explanation was made as to why the opening of the Congress itself, originally announced for the 25th, had been post- poned. Prime Minister CHOU En-lai reported the agenda for the Congress which included: (a) A report on the execution of the 1957 budget. (b) The draft of the 1958 budget. (c) The draft of the 1958 national economic plan. (d) The phonetic alphabet. (e) A readjustment of organizations under the State Council. The addition of the fifth item to those originally announced, presaging some reshuffling on the national. governmental level, seemed to strengthen the possiblity that the Congress would take some action against rightists in upper-level governmental posts. On 25 January, NCNA reported the preparatory meeting presided over by LIU Shao-chi. PENG Chen, a vice-chairman of the Standing Committee, member of the Politburo and Mayor of Peking, reported an agenda identical with that announced by CHOU En-lai on the 24th. The formal opening of the Fifth Session of the NPC was scheduled for 1 February. On 31 January, NCNA again announced the opening of the Congress on 1 February and listed an agenda which, without explanation, omitted the item on reorganization of organizations under the Slate Council. It is possible to speculate on the meaning of the alternate omission and inclusion of this item in the agenda as indicating in- decision or friction within the Chinese Communist leadership over the cabinet shuffle which was to take place. Reorganization of the government was again omitted. II. Removal of Rightists From Ministries Even before the Congress formally convened, the expected purge of rightists from upper-level government posts began. According to the Chinese Home Service of the Peking radio, the State Council on 31 January, after hearing a report from CHOU En-tai, voted to recommend to the Standing Committee of the NPC the dismissal of three non-CCP ministers who had been named in 1957 as rightists. The three were CHANG Po-chain, Minister of Communications, CHANG Nai-ch'i, Minister of Food, and LO Lung-chi, Minister of Timber Industry. The radio report did not detail the charges against these ministers which were presumably part of CHOU's report, but the dismissals came as no surprise. These three had been among the most outspoken critics of the party and system in the period of "blooming and contending" during the first half of 1957, and had been the principal targets of the anti- rightist campaign thereafter. The three rightist ministers, non-Communist Party members, had been representatives in the government of the so-called "demo- cratic parties", part of the facade of democracy provided by the Chinese Communists for their government. CHANG Po-chain and LO Lung-chi were Vice-chairmen of the Central Committee of the China Democratic League. CHANG Po-chain was also Chairman of the Central Committee of the Peasant-Worker Democratic Party. CHANG Nai-ch'i was Vice-chairman of the Central Com- mittee of the China Democratic National Construction Association and Vice-chairman of the All-China Federation of Industry and Commerce. The democratic parties had been targets for anti- rightist denunciation for their bourgeois ideology, failure to give wholehearted support to the building of socialism and for harboring rightists and counterrevolutionaries in their ranks. In self-defense and as a preliminary to the removal of the three ministers from their government posts, the democratic parties, between the open- ing of the preparatory meeting on 25 January and the convening of the NPC on 1 February had moved to purify themselves by expelling from office and membership in their parties a number of rightists, including the three ministers. Thus, since they no longer re- presented these parties, their removal from government office could be justified on constitutional as well as ideological grounds. The Standing Committee of the NPC confirmed the decision of the State Council and, accordingly, Chairman MAO on 31 January issued a formal decree dismissing the three rightists from their ministerial posts. Other Reports to the NPC On 3 February, the NPC heard reports from Finance Minister LI Hsien-nien on the budget and from Chairman of the National Economic Commission PO I-po on the National Economic Plan for 1958. It is not the purpose of this paper to attempt any sort of an analysis of the Chinese Communist economy based upon these reports. The generalized figures and statistics provided would not lend themselves to such an analysis in any case. However, there are a few implications in these reports which should be mentioned. LI Hsien-nien did admit that in the winter of 1956 there had been some overspending on capital construction. Of course, the Central Committee and MAO Tse-tung had corrected this error, The very general figures given in the draft budget for 1958 indicate that while a slight increase is planned for capital investment in agriculture, the principal emphasis will still be placed upon heavy industry. The total budget represents an increase of approximately three billion yuan over 1957. PO I-pots report also acknowledged the role of assistance from "the Soviet Union and other fraternal countries" in the accom- plishment of the first five-year plan and assured the Congress that "generous help" would continue to be forthcoming from the same sources. However, there is also throughout the report, as in other reports and speeches, a strong emphasis upon "practice economy and oppose waste", and repeated admonitions that only extreme frugality can make possible the fulfillment of the plan. The budget for 1958, as reported by LI Hsien-nien, does not include any figures for foreign aid in its tabulation of expected revenue, although such aid may be hidden in receipts from "foreign trade", "loans", or "other sources". Similarly, on the expenditure side no specific figure for repayment of foreign loans is given, although the total for repayment of loans of all kinds is 3. 12 percent of the total budget, an increase of 123. 01 percent over the 1957 figure. Rectification The progress of the rectification campaign and the anti-rightist movement was not part of the agenda of the N.PC. Perhaps it was felt that the report on the cheng feng movement delivered by TENG H.siao-p'ing to the plenary session of the Central Committee of the CCP on 23 September 1957 had adequately dealt with this question. Nevertheless, most of the speakers found it desirable to in- clude a reference to rectification in their remarks. CI 9U's report on foreign affairs began with a tribute to the campaign/having "infused an unprecedented new spirit" into the Chinese people. PO I-po credited rectification with having won "great victories on the political and ideological fronts" which in turn "spurred on victory on the economic front". The rectification campaign and the anti-rightist struggle, he went on, "constitute a socialist revolution on the political and ideological fronts and a continuation of the socialist revolution that took place in 1955-56 on the economic front. " PO also laid emphasis on the importance of practicing economy and opposing waste if eco- nomic goals were to be met. Such references to the success of rectification and anti-rightism could be multiplied from the speeches of the deputies at the NPC who seemed to feel that their own ideological purity required such state- ments crediting the campaigns with the successes and achievements in all fields.
