Dominated by Pre- mier Chou En-lai and his allies, the five-oa;y affair (Ja'ruary 13-17) stressed the twin themes of moderation and continuity. Its brevity as well as the secrecy surrounding it, however, indicate that significant differences remain within the Chinese leadership. For the military, the congress meant new cuts in its political clout. Politburo member Yeh Chien-ying, a close associate of Chou who can be expected to keep a close watch on the army, was named minister of defense. The post itself was downgraded somewhat by not making the defense minister a vice premier. Only one military man was among the 12 vice premiers named at the congress-there were four officers before the Cultural Revolution-and the party plenum that preceded the congress stripped another senior military figure, the much criticized Li Te-sheng, of his party vice chairmanship and his seat on the Politburo standing committee. Li's powerful posts went to a civilian, Vice Premier Teng Hsiao-ping, Chou's associate and heir apparent. The congress restaffed the top levels of the civilian bureaucracy. For the first time since the Cultural Revolution, each ministry has a publicly identified head. Perhaps more importantly, ex- pertise apparently was the chief criterion in filling Page 1 the vacancies. The majority of the 15 new min- isters are veteran bureaucrats, a number of whom were severely criticized during the Cultural Revolution. Only the new minister of culture can be considered a "leftist." Current moderate economic policies and China's expanded trade relations with the West were also endorsed by the congress. Trade policy, in particular, has been criticized by the leftists. Chou's report to the congress indicated that the demands of some military men for a bigger piece of the economic pie were also rejected. Concessions were made to the leftists in two areas. The right to practice the "four bigs"-es- sentially, permission to criticize leaders in wall posters-was written into the new constituticn, but was mitigated by the emphasis Chou's report placed on unity, discipline, and party control. Provincial revolutionary committees, the admin- istrative organs of local government, were also retained. A child of the Cultural Revolution, these committees replaced the older People's Councils, which many moderates would probably prefer to see reconstituted. Once a hotbed of leftist sentiment, the revolutionary committees have cooled considerably since they were formed and, in any case, the real power rests with the provincial party committees, where moderates generally rule. SECRET Eastern Europe REACTION TO US TRADE BILL East Europeans have generally followed the Soviet lead in blaming "anti-detente forces" in Washington for restrictions in the 1974 trade bill that caused Moscow to renounce the 1972 US- USSR trade agreement. The Kremlin's more loyal allies call it a temporary setback, however, and they point to the strategic arms limitation talks, the conference on European security, and imple- mentation of bilateral agreements as evidence that detente is a continuing process. Despite this up- beat note, some differences in national ap- proaches hr-e emerged. Reaction by Albania, Poland, Yugoslavia, and Romania reflects the particular national circumstances of each. . Albania factually reported Secretary Kis- singer's announcement of the Soviet decision, and then predictably condemned the "revisionist rulers" in the Kremlin for their willingness "to sell thousands and tens of thousands of their citizens for US credits and goods." By contrast, Polish media have barely noted the Soviet re- jection of the trade agreement. Warsaw's cov- erage-the most favorable to the US in Eastern Europe-has stressed the positive, including Secre- tary Kissinger's statement that the administration wil I urge Congress to reconsider the trade bill. Bucharest's failure to give even factual cov- erage suggests it is having difficulty finding a safe middle ground. The Romanians are now deeply involved in negotiating new trade agreements with both the US and the USSR. The Yugoslavs were slow to comment, perhaps because Moscow's action immediately preceded announcements in Belgrade and Wash- ington that President Ford has accepted an invitation to visit Yugoslavia. Influential news- papers in Belgrade have recently stated that any other decision by Moscow would have meant capitulation. Yugoslav journalists have, however, carefully placed the blame on "those quarters" in Washington that have blocked moves by the US administration to expand economic cooperation with Moscow and the Communist countries.
