Washington, D.C. 20006-(202) 638-4006 OCTOBER 1976 B-1 Status The B-1 bomber is further delayed. On September 1 House- Senate conferees on defense appropriations voted to delay funding of the bomber until February 1, 1977, when the next President will be in office. In the interim, money will be appropriated to permit the management and engineering staff to keep up the operation. The fate of the B-1 bomber hangs on a political thread. Gerald Ford and the Republican Party Platform favor proceed- ing with construction of the badly needed B-1. It is believed that If Jimmy Carter is elected, the B-1 program will not pro- ceed. This should most definitely be understood by the voters and should affect their selection of a President. In analyzing Carter's meteoric popularity it should be recalled that, when compared with Udall and Harris, he appeared initially to be Conservative. He won with only 30%-35% of the vote-rather than great major- ities-in the primaries, so his backing is obviously very thin. Never mind what he says; notice what he did do. The overall record is pretty dismal. The voice of the people was not as loud in the Halls of Congress as it should have been. Measured in terms of the nation's priorities, the record must be deemed nonproductive. In the matter of Tax Reform, the results will confound and confuse tax lawyers for many months to come. There was great talk about loopholes and inequities, but the 1,732-page Senate Bill adds further complications to an already overcom- plicated situation. In the matter of Energy, we find the same lack of program or progress. The President's plea for energy independence went unanswered. Research and development of sources be- sides oil were bogged down in bureaucratic bungling. The House voted not to consider a program designed to produce oil and gas from shale, coal gasification, and liquefaction be- cause it might delay the Members' departure for home. Some Members were more interested in breaking up or nationaliz- Ing the oil companies than in reducing the impediments to discoveries of new sources of domestic oil and gas. Result: In- creased cost and dependence on foreign oil. Programs designed to fight Crime got little or no attention. The Bail Reform Act, an improved drug enforcement law, and a recodification of the Federal Criminal Laws never came out of the Judiciary Committee. On the General Economy, only Presidential vetoes saved us taxpayers from a myriad of pork barrel boondoggles and phoney public works measures. This Congress talks economy but votes for Increased spending. All told, the Congress played at politics with added "over- sight" Committees challenging every Executive function while neglecting to properly clean up some of its own "in- house mess." With reporters swarming the halls and TV cameras grind- Ing, Congressional Committees and Subcommittees vied for public attention and the public problems went unattended. We hope the people will give Members of Congress their full attention come election time. MAINLAND CHINA As we prophesized months ago, Mainland China is now in a turmoil because of the death of Mao Tse-tung. It is obvious that the Communist stronghold never really extended beyond the big cities and was usually enforced by terrorism and strength. The swing now is apparently away from Communism and more to middle-of-the-road programs that will per- mit economic development. This is on the good side be- cause we can be of greater help to the recovery of China than any other country. On the bad side, however, is the possibility of a military take-over to solve the turmoil, which would greatly increase the chance of China and Russia join- ing forces against us. This is one of the problems of detente which has worried us at American Cause from the very beginning. If the two great Communist na- tions join forces, it could be most dangerous for the free world. Other good news is the fact that those responsible for the so-called Cultural Revolution, during which time approximately 50 million Chinese lost their lives, have been placed in jail and apparently are to be purged. More bad news, however, is in the fact that many parts of China are suffering food shortages, vandalism is on the upswing, and the general condition of the people is not really as happy as the propagandists would have us believe. The number of refugees leav- ing the mainland by way of Hong Kong continues to increase each year. It would seem ideal if the new rulers on Mainland China could rid themselves of the evil effects of Mao's Communism and pattern their society more after the plan of Taiwan. Les Aspin (Wis.), long-time Pentagon critic, probably summed it up best when he conceded that Soviet arms' spending "cannot be ignored."
