Declassified in Part - Sanitized Copy Approved for Release 2012/05/22: CIA-RDP87-01104R000100110001-9 UNITED STATES FORCES, JAPAN FOREIGN BROADCAST INFORMATION SERVICE OKINAWA BUREAU APO SAN FRANCISCO 96239 MOK-7001 7 January 1987 MEMORANDUM FOR: Director, Foreign Broadcast Information Service Chief, Operations Group, FBIS Acting Chief, inawa Bureau, FBIS STAT SUBJECT: Monthly Report - Okinawa Bureau - December 1986 A. Chinese student demonstrations for "democracy" and liberalization, the most interesting and perhaps most significant development in coverage of China since the Cultural Revolution of the 1960's, got off to a modest and uncertain start early in December, but by month-end became the primary focus of Chinese coverage. Initial reporting from AFP of 9 December was followed by Japan's KYODO and press coverage and more AFP, with PRC media themselves not acknowledging the demonstrations until the third week of December. The bureau laid on all-source coverage--Japanese agencies and television, open watches on Shanghai radio to catch any first reports, which were not monitored until 22 December, Beijing domestic radio, Beijing television, Xinhua Chinese press service, regional radios, as well as external services from Taiwan, to confirm whether these broadcasts had engaged in calls for insurrection on the mainland. Coverage exclusives included processing of a hard-to-read manuscript handbill--calling for "the overthrow of Deng Xiaoping and his ilk" shown on Beijing television--alleged to be in the possession of a "counterrevolutionary" ringleader arrested in late December, by using video freeze-frame techniques to decipher the hand-lettered document. While at year-end new demonstrations were announced, and authoritative comment became more frequent and outspoken against the demonstrators, coverage to date suggests the demonstrations likely will continue into the new year, if perhaps on a smaller scale as authorities began taking a stronger stand and making more use of police. Despite sometimes uncertain bureau handling in the early days, the American Consulate in Shanghai praised the "superb" support from Okinawa in providing "our most important and timely source of reporting on the demonstrations," and offered "heartfelt thanks" and "congratulations on a job well done." C/OPs promised a sobering "lump of coal" for Christmas from DRD's managing editor to point out areas where hindsight indicated things could have been done a bit better. B. The first pay increase for the bureau's monitor and associate editor staff in two years, a 3 percent raise effective 4 January 1987, was assured after exchanges in early December with the FBIS Administrative Staff and PMCD over the new pay scale. Okinawa's schedule is tied to the U.S. GS scale minus 15 percent, and when a 1986 pay increase was rejected for U.S. staff, bureau TCN's also had failed to get one. -- Declassified in Part - Sanitized Copy Approved for Release 2012/05/22 : CIA-RDP87-01104R000100110001-9 Declassified in Part - Sanitized Copy Approved for Release 2012/05/22 : CIA-RDP87-01104R000100110001-9 C. The depth of administrative continuity at Okinawa was proved during December when most of the senior managers were absent for most or all of the month for various kinds of leave. In a series of handovers resembling "Tinker to Evers to Chance," the bureau chief left on 29 November for an extended medical evaluation in Washington, the deputy/acting chief handed over to the chief editor on 5 December, the chief editor/acting chief handed over to the regional engineer on 19 December, and the regional engineer/acting chief handed back to the returned deputy chief/acting chief on 28 December. While some unease over this was conveyed. politely in Headquarters, in fact bureau staffers and local employees carried on and things got done on schedule. Holiday absences have become much more popular since rest and recuperation travel to the United States became possible two years ago. A. Monitorial/Editorial 1. A 25 December KYODO agency report claiming that the Moscow-controlled clandestine Chinese-language radio Ba Yi had been discontinued confirmed bureau observations that the radio had stopped broadcasting since 2 December, possibly as part of a Soviet effort to improve relations with Beijing. Cruising rechecked Ba Yi through December and will continue to do so at AG's request until the end of January, when AG plans to discuss the latest suspension of Ba Yi. No evidence was found of current broadcasts by another purported Soviet-controlled radio, Red Flag, although our chief monitor found a 1983 reference to it in a Japanese DXers' guide. 2. Okinawa's role in coverage of Vietnam's sixth party congress proved to be minor, consistent with our single Vietnamese monitor and continued erratic reception of the NHAN DAN satellite facsimile transmission. We were able to supply substantial processing from VNA English transmissions, working closely with Bangkok, on Truong Chinh's major political report on 15-16 December, and our lone Vietnamese monitor provided extensive coverage from Moscow and Beijing Vietnamese broadcasts on the congress. The satellite NHAN DAN pressfax signal yielded only a few legible items that were fragments of low-level speeches at the CPV Congress. Okinawa did supply additional passages in the speech by the USSR's delegation leader, Ligachev, as carried by Moscow radio, from a Hanoi-to-Moscow satellite feed on the 80 East satellite, including a passage on "direct dialogue" between China and Vietnam. 3. Tests of the fourth and final model of Japanese-made facsimile machines were completed, with Headquarters reporting all models provided excellent transmission. Purchase of a facsimile unit will be deferred until we receive word on its potential use in any Okinawa role in relieving Hong Kong Bureau of some of its editorial workload. 2. As at other bureaus, Okinawa is applying a "sneaker" network to its IBM computers, by hand-carrying floppy disks around, to reduce rekeying of many kinds of material, especially lengthy cruising messages with columns of figures, monthly financial status reports, and increasingly for editor-generated service messages and FYI's, in advance of the scheduled "Quick Reaction Bureau" local area network scheduled for installation this summer. 3. Visiting communications engineers inspected the new secure communications vault, pronounced it in excellent condition, and the new gear for installation in mid-January is expected any day, with training to follow the week of 18 January. 4. Our regional engineer has continued experimentation with capture of a major English press agency file, Xinhua, on IBM PC floppy disk storage to simplify and speed retrieval of selected items to be processed. The equipment is being used on a trial basis by several operators, who have grown to prefer it over hand searches in boxes of paper tapes for the correct Xinhua items. A memo on the equipment and process was prepared for C/ESG and its components. C. The FBIS Motor Pool at Kadena AFB became hostage to U.S. Air Force Security Police for more than 3 hours on 5 December, involuntarily, when police decided to use the FBIS facilities as an operations base in a mock "hostage" incident created by Air Force inspectors at the 313th Air Division Headquarters building just across the main road. The street was blocked and several hundred military personnel took over the FBIS office, kitchen and community room to deal with "armed gunmen" holding hostages at the 313th, including base commander Colonel Gill. In the haste to depart, the security police inadvertently abandoned one of their vehicles in the Motor Pool. An informal party was held on 15 December at the bureau to mark 35 years of service with FBIS for associate editor A congratulatory letter from the C/Operations Grou express n-g congratulations from himself, the D/FBIS and many friends was read, and a cake created in own image was consumed by the large number of employees who attended.
