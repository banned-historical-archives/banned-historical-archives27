Polish and Soviet leaders yesterday concluded their two-day summit with a communique in which the Poles re- newed their loyalty to Moscow and to "socialism" and pledged to "cut short resolutely" any future efforts to disrupt Poland's economic and political system. Meanwhile, unidentified Soviet sources have told Western reporters that the USSR agreed during Jaruzelski's visit to provide Poland with more aid than was called for in the protocol of early January. The sources did not indicate how much additional aid would be provided, or in what form. Comment: The communique, as well as the visit as a whole, is reminiscent of the pre-Solidarity era. USSR - EAST ASIA: Soviet Peace Proposals President Brezhnev has broadened Soviet arms con- trol propaganda to include East Asia and the Pacific. The Soviet leader, in a wide-ranging letter responding to an appeal by an Australian group to curtail the arms race, recently proposed that the US and USSR limit mili- tary activities in the Pacific. In replying to a similar appeal from Japanese intellectuals, Brezhnev on Monday proposed a formal bilateral agreement that Moscow would not use nuclear arms against Japan in return for a pro- hibition by the Japanese against nuclear warheads on their territory. Comment: The suggestion for curbing military activ- ities in the Pacific, which parallels the Soviet proposal for the Indian Ocean, underscores Moscow's interest in encouraging public support for regional security talks in the western Pacific, particularly in Japan. Brezhnev's proposal will embarrass Prime Minister Suzuki. Even though Suzuki will reject it in order not to put a further strain on Japan's security relationship with the US, he will find it difficult to present a rationale that will be persuasive for the many Japanese opposed to nuclear weapons.
