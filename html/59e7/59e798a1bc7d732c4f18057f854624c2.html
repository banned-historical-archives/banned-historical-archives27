A senior economist in Moscow's Institute for World Socialist Economy recently told a US embassy officer that policies agreed upon at a CEW summit this fall will be aligned with "theses" for the 25th CPSU Congress to be held in February. He said that drafting of the theses has now been completed, and that they are being reviewed at various levels, in- cluding the CPSU Central Committee Secretariat and the "Central Committee itself." He predicted that the theses will probably be published in the Soviet press in November, or possibly earlier. This is the first report we have had of theses for the 25th congress. "Theses" are not usually published in connection with a party congress; there were none for either of the last two (1966 and 1971). "Theses" are usually reserved for major landmark occasions--for example, the 100th anniversary of Lenin's birth in 1970, or the 50th anniversary of the Bolshevik Revolution. Khrushchev, on the other hand, used them to launch public discussion of his educational reform. If the economist is right and there are, indeed, "theses" being prepared for the 25th congress, the event may be presented in an unusually dramatic framework as an historic landmark. The 22nd Congress in 1961, among other things, approved a visionary Party Program, mapping out the stages toward reaching communism in the coming decades. This program, bearing Khrushchev's stamp, has been an embarrassment to the current leadership since Khrushchev's ouster. Brezhnev and others have shown some interest in giving this congress--probably his last--a similarly programmatic character that would put their stamp on the future and replace the 1961 program..
