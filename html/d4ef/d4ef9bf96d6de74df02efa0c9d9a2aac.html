SOVIET LEADERS WORK ON ECONOMIC PROBLEMS Events in the Soviet Union this past week showed the lead- ership concerned to a high de- gree with working out solutions to current economic problems and with charting the future course of economic development. Con- sideration apparently is being given to some new policies-- possibly concerning greater ma- terial incentives. Top-Level Meeting On 28 February an unusual top-level meeting was convened in Moscow ostensibly.to find ways of implementing the de- cisions of the central committee plenum on agriculture which had ended two weeks earlier. Party presidium members, party secre- taries, deputy chairmen of the USSR Council of Ministers, as well as various republic-level and local officials heard Khru- shchev once again castigate Stalin's neglect of agriculture .and reaffirm the new fertilizer and irrigation programs. The brief press summary of Khrushchev's report gives no clues as to how the regime may intend to improve farm incentives, although this was evidently an important topic both at last week's meeting and at the agri- cultural plenum. He again called for greater central control, particularly to ensure adequate equipment and machinery for chemical projects. The importance of the as- semblage at last week's meeting contrasted sharply with the com- plete lack of novelty in the issues summarized in the press release. Arzumanyan Article Probably also related to Moscow's current concern with economic matters was the wide- ranging article by the well-known economist A. Arzumanyan which appeared in Pravda of 24 and 25 February. Invoking Lenin at fre- quent intervals, Arzumanyan set out an elaborate defense of the current Khrushchev economic pro- gram. He then leveled a compre- hensive attack on "Stalinist" adherence to heavy industry for its sake alone and on economic planning which does not favor those branches that support tech- nological advance but, rather, programs all industries to de- velop "arithmetically" with no regard to their economic contri- bution. Neither of these oversim- plifications is new---Khruschchev has often used them in dicuss- ing the need for greater re- sources for agriculture and the consumer goods program. Arzuman- yan, however, extended his argu- ments well beyond any point to which Khrushchev has publicly committed himself. According to Arzumanyan, the unconditional development of heavy industry (group "A" in Soviet jargon) is not necessarily a requisite of rapid growth dur- ing all phases of economic his- tory. Citing the US as an ex- ample, he attempted to show that there can be rapid technological development even though the out- put of consumer goods (group "B") is rising much more rapidly then that of heavy industry. Policy concerning the planned growth rates of "A" in- dustries and "B" industries has been a sensitive economic issue in recent years, symbolizing the whole question of how resources are to be allocated to the vari- ous claimants. Since Stalin, consumer goods production has been emphasized more, but heavy industry continues to receive overwhelming priority in economic planning. said that in the future light and heavy industry would develop at the same rate. Khrushchev probably is not satisfied with this formula. At last month's plenum, he affirmed party support for the develop- ment of heavy industry but strongly asserted that the out- put of consumer goods must grow more rapidly in the future. Tighter Labor Controls Proposed Some action was suggested last week in another area of con- cern to the regime with the ap- pearance of a letter in Izvestia urging that the worker's labor record become a principal docu- ment of identification, replacing the internal passport. A worker presumably would be deterred from frequent job-changing by embar- rassment at having this made a matter of important record. In turn, the labor card could be the basis for preferential treat- ment and reward for longevity. This proposal is obviously intended to reinstate some control over what has become a relatively "free" labor market and encourage workers--particularly in agr.cul- ture--to stay in the specialties for which they were trained. How- ever, such a rule, which could reduce labor turnover somewhat, would still be far milder than the Draconian labor laws of Sta- lin's time which virtually froze 25X1 a worker to a particular job at the discretion of the regime. BLOC'S FRIENDSHIP OIL PIPELINE ONE YEAR BEHIND SCHEDULE Although Communist Europe has heralded the opening of its Friendship crude-oil pipeline, the main 40-inch section will not be completed until late 1964, almost one year behind the original sched- ule. (see map). The delay is due largely to a shift in priority to construc- tion of gas pipelines. Most of the 40-inch pipeline available in 1963 was used to complete on schedule the gas line from the Gazli field in Soviet Uzbekistan to Chelyabinsk in the Urals. In addition, some of the USSR's new 40-inch-pipe mills apparently failed to attain the expected rate of production. NATO's agreement not to sell large-diameter (partic- ularly 40-inch) pipe to the USSR indirectly helped delay construc- tion, but the USSR probably could have obtained pipe from the UK if completion of the Friendship line had been considered urgent. About 500 miles of 40-inch oil pipe are yet to be installed be- tween Penza and Unecha. Work also is under way on two major offshoots of the Friendship Pipeline. The first, the Unecha-Polotsk-Ventspils route, will carry crude oil to a large refinery being constructed at Polotsk and to the Baltic port of Ventspils for export to Northern Europe. Secondly, in Czechoslo- vakia, the central system is be- ing extended beyond Bratislava to the refinery-petrochemical com- plex at Zaluzi. Demand for oil is increasing rapidly in Eastern Europe, which has a major refinery construction progran under way.
