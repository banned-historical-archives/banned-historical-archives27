INTRODUCTION 1. There is insufficient reliable information on hand to give a detailed picture o-f the actual organization of the Lao Dong or Labor Party, the Com- munist Party of Viet Nam. What is contained herein is primarily the struc- ture of the Party as it is given in the Statutes. Z. The Statutes of the Lao Dong Party, which were framed in 1951 during the anti-French war in Indochina, are not absolutely comprehen- sive on organizational matters, and were probably so designed inten- tionally, to permit flexibility and growth. of the organization within the Communist framework which places all authority in the Party Center. The differences between the theoretical and actual state of organization of the Party are undoubtedly based upon the several "elastic clauses" of the Statutes, and are also due to the historical developments since the Statutes were written. The actual general outlines and organiza- tional principles of the Lao Dong Party are fairly similar to those of other Communist parties, particularly the Chfinese Communist Party. Some portions of the Statutes, notably those concerning the duties of the Central Committee, and the organization of the basic party unit, greatly resemble parts of the constitutions and statib?s of other Communist parties, and are couched in typical Communist phrases. U. PRINCIPLES OF ORGANIZATION 3. The Statutes of the Lao Dong Party provide for a pyramidal party structure based on the principle of "democratic centralism. " This principle in the Lao Dong Party Statutes -- as in the regulations of other parties -- calls for a highly centralized and authoritarian structure, in which in actuality, the "democratic" aspects inevitably deteriorate to little more than mock congresses and elections. 4. The Lao Dong Party Statutes stipulate that the greatest execu- tive and administrative power at each echelon of party organization, above the party's basic organizational unit, is supposed to be exercised by a representative body composed of representatives elected by the party's -1- Approved For Release : CIA-RDP78-00915R000400450005-3 Approved For Release : CIA-RDP78-00915R000400450005-3 next lower echelon. This body in turn theoretically elects a party com- mittee for that particular echelon, to wield the power at that echelon be- tween the regular sessions of the body which elected it. The Statutes also state that all decisions and activities of party authorities at a particular organizational level, with the exception of the highest level, are to be subject to the directives and approval of party authorities at the next higher level. Furthermore, the Lao Dong Party Statutes employ standard Com- munist terminology to. describe organizational components, such as "Cen- tral Committee", "Political Bureau, " and "basic party unit". The organiza- tional framework described by the party Statutes is clearly modelled after that of other Communist parties and are not based upon indigenous politi- cal institutions. Power centers in the Party 6. Since the establishment of the Lao Dong Party in 1951, the actual party organization has become more authoritarian and personalized than the nominal structure specified in the Statutes.. The "representative bodies" at the various party echelons hav met very infrequently, and the preponderance of governing power within the Party has devolved into a hierarchy of party committees, headed by the Central Committee, the Politburo, and the Secretariat (whose existence is assumed but not certain). The party committees at all party echelons generally have con- trolled the organization. and activities of the next lower echelon, for the higher echelon has the statutory responsibility of approving the selection of members of the party committee at the next lower echelon. In practice, authority nominally residing in the party committee has sometimes become Approved For Release : CIA-RDP78-00915R000400450005-3 Approved For Release : CIA-RDP78-00915R000400450005-3 concentrated in smaller bodies or in individual leaders. For example, -some large basic party units have been. headed, according to reports, by individuals instead of by committees as prescribed by the party Statutes. TlrStatutes require basic party units having more than nine members to have an elected party committee to administer the policy and programs of the arty and unit. Only small basic party units have the statutory right to omit the party committee and to elect only a secretary to head the unit. 7. Despite the highly centralized natiLre of the formal party struc- ture it probably has not as yet become as monolithic as desired by the top party, leaders, who openly desire to create an organization in Viet Nam with the monolithic characteristics of Communist parties in other countries. Several instances of independent action by some local party leaders have occurred in the past years. These incidents were probably caused by widespread guerrilla warfare and the inadequate communication facilities which prevented tight application of party discipline by the Center. Even before the beginning of large scale warfare in Indochina in 1946, .the Communist apparatus in South Viet Nam clearly enjoyed greater administra- tive autonomy than did the Communist structure in other regions of Viet Nam, and as recently as 19541 southernCommunist leaders' clearly voiced objections to the Geneva settlement showed a failure to quickly transmit the party line from the Party Center. Whether any autonomy continues to exist among party organizations in the South is a vital question. 8. Although there is some evidence that the party chain of command in North Viet Nam is. not as yet as centralized as the .party leaders desire and proper party organizations would require, no inference should be made from this data that there is a lack of party unity. It is true that there are visible party cliques of those leaders who were.associated with the Com- munist Party of China and those nurtured by the Communist Party of France, and potential sources for friction could derive from decisions on economic development or how to gain control of South Viet Nam. However, such present divisions or future disputes should not be given too much weight. M. TERRITORIAL ORGANIZATION OF THE PARTY 9. Very little reliable information is available concerning the actual composition and functions of the individual party echelons. The important party offices are now located in Hanoi. Before the armistice in Indochina in 1954, several major party agencies reportedly had headquarters in the town of Thai Nguyen, approximately 60 miles north of Hanoi. 13. As is found in the Statutes of other Communist parties, the Statutes of the Lao Dong Party name as the most important party bodies the National Party Congress, the National Party Conference, the Cen- tral Committee, the Political Bureau, and the office of the Secretary- General. A :French study, dated 1951, also names these bodies as the most influential bodies of the party. In practice, the National Party Congress does not have the influence or power ascribed to it by the Statutes of the Lao Dong Party, and should not be included with the other named bodies as a body having real political power. Central Committee 18. The Central Committee (CC) of the Lao Dong Party is theoretically a very influential party body. According to the party Statutes, the CC is the "organ of highest authority" in the Party between sessions of the National Party Congress. The actual powers, however, seem to reside more in the spheres of ratifying plans, issuing broad policy directives, sand formal. liaison with the formal government structure and with other non-party centers of power vai:n::theregime, rather than in the executive direction of. day-to-day party affairs. The party Statutes invest the CC with several important powers including ratification of the decisions of the National Party Conference, super- vision of "all activities of the party within the national domain", enforcement.; of the party stattltes and policies, and the election of the Secretariat, the Secretary-General, the- Politburo, the Central Control Committee, and the members of the party's central departments. Nevertheless, no reliable information is available concerning the actual extent to which the Central Committee exercises its statutory powers. Probably, the Central Com- mittee exercises its powers in a nominal manner only, by receiving and perfunctorily approving periodic reports from the other policy organs of the Party. Such is the practice in many Communist parties. Although the - Central Committee is a national party organ. and all directives for the Party are issued in its name, the actual power it exercises is limited. It is primarily a "rubber stamp" group, since it does not have the real ability to discuss and question party policy and practice with any hope of modifica- tion, or change. To judge by the Statutes and scattered intelligence reports, the party organization on these lower levels bears a general resemblance to the party organization on the central level. The Statutes of the Lao Dong Party provide that the highest authority of the "region" or "zone" is held by the "Zonal Party Congress.-, which is composed of "elected" representatives of lesser party elements. The Zonal Party Congresses are required by party statute to meet each eighteen. months, but like all of the various party congresses, from the lowest level to the national level, they have met less frequently than ostensibly required. In general, the Zonal. Party Congress, like the other party congresses on the various levels, possesses little actual power in the affairs of the Party. 41. One of the principal functions of the Zonal Party Congress is to elect a Zonal Party Committee which for the particular area resembles the Central Committee. By party statute, the Zonal. Party Committee is "the organ of highest authority" between sessions of the Zonal Party Congress. BASIC PARTY ORGANIZATIONAL UNITS 47. The organization -of a basic party organizational unit evidently depends on its size. According to the Party Statutes, units of less than, nine regular members are;supposed to be headed only by a "secretary" and possibly a "deputy secretary", while larger units are theoretically directed by a party committee of three to seven members, as well as a secretary and deputy secretary. The full unit membership is supposed to elect a party committee and/or secretary. From reports it appears that the majority of basic party units have party committees. 48. A basic party unit can be organized, according to the Statutes, in any locality where a minimum of three party members are sufficiently close to each other to hold meetings, such as in. a commercial enter- prise, school, or village, and where the necessary approval of higher party authorities is obtained. In some areas where party members are numerous, the basic party unit may be divided into sub-units and placed under the direction of a sub-unit leader. In such cases, the sub-unit and the sub-unit leader remains responsible to the basic party organiza- tional unit. Each basic party unit is supposed to hold plenary sessions once a month to hear reports on the progress of its party missions, to deliberate on directives from higher party levels, to fix new goals and to allocate new tasks, and to decide on the admission of new members. Party Statutes decree that the membership of basic party units in a commercial enterprise should not exceed seventy members, while in a town, or city precinct, the total membership of a basic party unit should not exceed fifty. There is no xeliable evidence about the total number of basic party units and it is undoubtedly constantly changing. It has been repeatedly stated, and recently reemphasized, that the basic party units in rural areas are the backbone of the Lao Dong Party in Viet Nam, a position similar to that taken by the Chinese Communist Party and the Communist Party of Indonesia.
